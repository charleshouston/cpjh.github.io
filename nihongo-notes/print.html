Expressing state-of-being

Declaring that something is so using 「だ」

Attach 「だ」to the noun or na-adjective.

Examples

  1. 人だ。

    Is person.

  2. 学生だ。

    Is student.

  3. 元気だ。

    Is well.

Typical casual greeting

A state-of-being can be implied without using 「だ」!

A: 元気？

A: (Are you) well?

B: 元気。

B: (I'm) well.

Conjugating to the negative state-of-being

Attach 「じゃない」to the noun or na-adjective.

Examples

  1. 学生じゃない。

    Is not student.

  2. 友達じゃない。

    Is not friend.

  3. 元気じゃない。

    Is not well.

Conjugating to the past state-of-being

Past state-of-being: Attach 「だった」to the noun or na-adjective.

Negative past state-of-being: Conjugate the noun or na-adjective to the negative first and then replace 「い」of 「じゃない」with 「かった」.

Examples

  1. 学生だった。

    Was student.

  2. 友達じゃなかった。

    Was not friend.

  3. 元気じゃなかった。

    Was not well.

Introduction to Particles

The 「は」topic particle

The topic particle identifies what it is that you're talking about, essentially the topic of your sentence.

Examples

ボブ：アリスは学生？

Bob: Is Alice (you) student?

アリス：うん、学生。

Alice: Yeah, (I) am.

ボブ：ジョンは明日？

Bob: John is tomorrow?

アリス：ううん、明日じゃない。

Alice: No, not tomorrow.

アリス：今日は試験だ。

Alice: Today is exam.

ボブ：ジョンは？

Bob: What about John?

アリス：ジョンは明日。

Alice: John is tomorrow. (As for John, the exam is tomorrow.)

The 「も」inclusive topic particle

The inclusive particle is essentially the same as the topic particle with the additional meaning "also". Basically, it can introduce another topic in addition to the current topic.

Examples

ボブ：アリスは学生？

Bob: Is Alice (you) student?

アリス：うん、トムも学生.

Alice: Yeah, and Tom is also student.

ボブ：アリスは学生？

Bob: Is Alice (you) student?

アリス：うん、でもトムは学生じゃない。

Alice: Yeah, but Tom is not student.

ボブ：アリスは学生？

Bob: Is Alice (you) student?

アリス：ううん、トムも学生じゃない。

Alice: No, and Tom is also not student.

The 「が」identifier particle

The identifier particle is used to indicate the speaker wants to identify something unspecified.

Examples

ボブ：誰が学生？

Bob: Who is the one that is student?

アリス：ジョンが学生。

Alice: John is the one who is student.

  1. 誰が学生？

    Who is the one that is student?

  2. 学生は誰？

    (The) student is who?

The first sentence seeks to identify a specific person for "student" while the second sentence is simply talking about the student.

  1. 私は学生。

    I (am) student.

  2. 私が学生。

    I (am) student.

In the first sentence, since 「私」is the topic, the sentence means, "Speaking about me, I am a student". However, the second sentence is specifying who 「学生」is - if we want to know who the student is, the 「が」particle tells us that it's 「私」.

  1. 私は学生。

    As for me, (I am) student.

  2. 私が学生。

    I (am) the one (that is) student.

Adjectives

All adjectives fall under two categories: na-adjectives and i-adjectives.

The na-adjective

Conjugation rules for nouns and na-adjectives are the same. You can directly modify a noun following it by sticking 「な」between the adjective and noun.

Examples

  1. 静かな人。

    Quiet person.

  2. きれいな人。

    Pretty person.

You can also use adjectives with particles.

  1. 友達は親切。

    Friend is kind.

  2. 友達は親切な人だ。

    Friend is kind person.

The conjugation rules for na-adjectives are the same as nouns.

  1. ボブは魚が好きだ。

    Bob likes fish.

  2. ボブは魚が好きじゃない。

    Bob does not like fish.

  3. ボブは魚が好きだった。

    Bob liked fish.

  4. ボブは魚が好きじゃなかった。

    Bob did not like fish.

You can also use the last three conjugations to directly modify the noun.

  1. 魚が好きな人。

    Person that likes fish.

  2. 魚が好きじゃない人。

    Person that does not like fish.

  3. 魚が好きだった人。

    Person that liked fish.

  4. 魚が好きじゃなかった人。

    Person that did not like fish.

We can treat the whole descriptive noun as we would a single noun.

  1. 魚が好きじゃない人は、肉が好きだ。

    Person who does not like fish like meat.

  2. 魚が好きな人は、野菜も好きだ。

    Person who likes fish also like vegetables.

The i-adjective

All i-adjectives always end in the Hiragana character 「い」. Very few na-adjectives also end with 「い」, the most common of which are 「きれい」and 「嫌い」.

Examples

You do not need to add 「な」to directly modify a noun with an i-adjective.

  1. 嫌いな食べ物。

    Hated food.

  2. おいしい食べ物。

    Tasty food.

Conjugation rules for i-adjectives

Do NOT attach 「だ」to i-adjectives.

  • Negative: First remove the trailing 「い」from the i-adjective and then attach 「くない」.
  • Past-tense: First remove the trailing 「い」from the i-adjective or negative i-adjective and then attach 「かった」.

More Examples

  1. 高いビル。

    Tall building.

  2. 高くないビル。

    Not tall building.

  3. 高かったビル。

    Building that was tall.

  4. 高くなかったビル。

    Building that was not tall.

値段が高いレストランはあまり好きじゃない。

Don't like high price restaurants very much.

An annoying exception

Conjugation for 「いい」

  • Positive: いい
  • Negative: よくない
  • Past: よかった
  • Negative Past: よくなかった

Conjugation for 「かっこいい」

  • Positive: かっこいい
  • Negative: かっこよくない
  • Past: かっこよかった
  • Negative Past: かっこよくなかった

Verb Basics

Role of Verbs

Verbs, in Japanese, always come at the end of clauses.

A grammatically complete sentence requires a verb only (including state-of-being).

食べる。

Eat. (possible translations include: I eat/she eats/they eat)

Classifying verbs into ru-verbs and u-verbs

All ru-verbs end in 「る」while u-verbs can end in a number of u-vowel sounds including 「る」.

If a verb does not in in 「る」, it will always be a u-verb.

For verbs ending in 「る」:

  • /a/, /u/ or /o/ vowel sound, it will always be a u-verb.

  • /i/ or /e/ vowel sound, it will be a ru-verb in most cases.

  1. 食べる -「べ」 is an e-vowel sound so it is a ru-verb.
  2. 分かる -「か」 is an a-vowel sound so it is a u-verb.

Examples

  1. アリスは食べる。

    As for Alice, eat.

  2. ジムが来る。

    Jim is the one that comes.

  3. ボブもする。

    Bob also do.

  4. お金がある。

    There is money. (lit: Money is the thing that exists.)

  5. 私は買う。

    As for me, buy.

  6. 猫はいる。

    There is cat. (lit: As for cat, it exists.)

Negative Verbs

Conjugating verbs into the negative

A very important exception

「ある」(u-verb) - to exist (inanimate)

「いる」(ru-verb) - to exist (animate)

The negative of 「ある」 is 「ない」(meaning that something does not exist).

Conjugation rules for negative verbs

For ru-verbs: Drop the 「る」and attach 「ない」.

For u-verbs that end in「う」: Replace 「う」with「わ」and attach 「ない」.

For all other u-verbs: Replace the u-vowel sound with the a-vowel equivalent and attach 「ない」.

Exceptions:

  1. する - しない
  2. くる - こない
  3. ＊ある - ない

Examples

  1. アリスは食べない。

    As for Alice, does not eat.

  2. ジムが遊ばない。

    Jim is the one that does not play.

  3. ボブもしない。

    Bob also does not do.

  4. お金がない。

    There is no money. (lit: Money is the thing that does not exist.)

  5. 私は買わない。

    As for me, not buy.

  6. 猫はいない。

    There is no cat. (lit: As for cat, does not exist.)

Past Tense

Past tense for ru-verbs

Drop the 「る」part of the ru-verb and add 「た」.

Examples

  1. ご飯は、食べた。

    As for meal, ate.

  2. 映画は、全部見た。

    As for movie, saw them all.

Past tense for u-verbs

Four categories based on verb endings, and some exceptions.

For「す」endings: 「す」changes to 「した」.

For 「く」or 「ぐ」endings: 「く」changes to 「いた」and 「ぐ」changes to 「いだ」.

For 「む」,「ぶ」or 「ぬ」endings: 「む/ぶ/ぬ」changes to 「んだ」.

For 「る」,「う」or 「つ」endings: 「る/う/つ」changes to 「った」.

Exceptions:

  1. 「する」-「した」
  2. 「来る」-「来た」
  3. 「行く」-「いった」

Examples

  1. 今日は、走った。

    As for today, ran.

  2. 友達が来た。

    Friend is the one that came.

  3. 私も遊んだ。

    I also played.

  4. 勉強は、した。

    About study, did it.

Past-negative tense for all verbs

Change the verb to the negative and replace the 「い」with「かった」.

Examples

  1. アリスは食べなかった。

    As for Alice, did not eat.

  2. ジムがしなかった。

    Jim is the one that did not do.

  3. ボブも行かなかった。

    Bob also did not go.

  4. お金がなかった。

    There was no money. (lit: As for money, did not exist.)

  5. 私は買わなかった。

    As for me, did not buy.

  6. 猫はいなかった。

    There was no cat. (lit: As for cat, did not exist.)

Particles used with verbs

The direct object 「を」particle

The 「を」particle is attached to the end of a word to signify that that word is the direct object of the verb.

Examples

  1. 魚を食べる。

    Eat fish.

  2. ジュースを飲んだ。

    Drank juice.

Places can also be the direct object of motion verbs such as 「歩く」and「走る」.

  1. 街をぶらぶら歩く。

    Aimlessly walk through town. (lit: Aimlessly walk town.)

  2. 高速道路を走る。

    Run through expressway. (lit: Run expressway.)

When you use 「する」with a noun the 「を」particle is optional and you can treat the whole 「noun+する」as one verb.

  1. 毎日、日本語を勉強する。

    Study Japanese everyday.

  2. メールアドレスを登録した。

    Registered email address.

The target 「に」particle

The 「に」particle can specify the target of a verb.

Examples

  1. ボブは日本に行った。

    Bob went to Japan.

  2. 家に帰らない。

    Not go back home.

  3. 部屋に来る。

    Come to room.

The target particle always targets "to" rather than "from". 「から」can be used to say "come from". 「から」is often paired with 「まで」which means "up to".

  1. アリスは、アメリカから来た。

    Alice came from America.

  2. 宿題を今日から明日までする。

    Will do homework from today to tomorrow.

The idea of target in Japanese is not restricted to motion verbs.

  1. 猫は部屋にいる。

    Cat is in room.

  2. いすが台所にあった。

    Chair was in the kitchen.

  3. いい友達に会った。

    Met good friend.

  4. ジムは医者になる。

    Jim will become a doctor.

  5. 先週に図書館に行った。

    Went to library last week.

The use of the 「に」particle is optional with time but causes a slight difference in meaning. In the following examples, the target particle makes the date a specific target emphasizing that the friend will go to Japan at that time. Without the particle, there is no special emphasis.

  1. 友達は、来年、日本に行く。

    Next year, friend go to Japan.

  2. 友達は、来年に日本に行く。

    Friend go to Japan next year.

The directional 「へ」particle

While the 「に」particle goes to a target as the final, intended destination (both physical and abstract), the 「へ」particle is used to express the fact that one is setting out towards the direction of the target.

This particle is only used with directional motion verbs.

It does not guarantee whether the target will reach the final intended destination, only that it is heading towards that direction.

Examples

  1. ボブは日本へ行った。

    Bob headed towards Japan.

  2. 家へ帰らない。

    Not go home toward house.

  3. 部屋へ来る。

    Come towards room.

We cannot use the 「へ」particle with verbs that have no physical direction. However, it can be used to talk about setting out towards certain future goals and expectations.

  • 勝ちへ向かう。

    Go towards victory.

The contextual 「で」particle

The 「で」particle specifies the context in which the action is performed.

Examples

  1. 映画館で見た。

    Saw at movie theatre.

  2. バスで帰る。

    Go home by bus.

  3. レストランで昼ご飯を食べた。

    Ate lunch at restaurant.

Using 「で」with 「何」

The word for "what" (「何」) is usually read as 「なに」but sometimes it is read as 「なん」. In these examples it is read 「なにで」.

  1. 何できた？

    Came by the way of what?

  2. バスできた。

    Came by the way of bus.

However, there is a colloquial version of the word "why" that is used much more often than the less colloquial version 「どうして」or the more forceful 「なぜ」. It is also written as 「何で」but is read as 「なんで」. This is a completely separate word and nothing to do with the 「で」particle.

  1. 何できた？

    Why did you come?

  2. 暇だから。

    Because I am free (as in have nothing to do).

The 「から」here meaning "because" is different from the 「から」is the previous section and will be covered later.

When location is the topic

When the location of an action is also the topic of a sentence you can attach the topic particle 「は」and 「も」to the three particles that indicate location (「に」,「へ」,「で」).

Examples

ボブ：学校に行った？

Bob: (Did you) go to school?

アリス：行かなかった。

Alice: Didn't go.

ボブ：図書館には？

Bob: What about library?

アリス：図書館にも行かなかった。

Alice: Also didn't go to library.

ボブ：どこで食べる？

Bob: Eat where?

アリス：イタリアレストランではどう？

Alice: How about Italian restaurant?

When direct object is the topic

The direct object particle is different from particles related to location in that you cannot use any other particles at the same time.

Examples

  1. 日本語を習う。

    Learn Japanese.

  2. 日本語は、習う。

    About Japanese, (will) learn it.
